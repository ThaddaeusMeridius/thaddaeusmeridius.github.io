Green's Theorem

$\def\RR{\mathbb{R}}$ $\def\ZZ{\mathbb{Z}}$ $\def\NN{\mathbb{N}}$ $\def\CC{\mathbb{C}}$ $\newcommand{\v}[1]{\vec{\mathbf{#1}}}$ $\newcommand{\(}[1]{\left( #1 \right)}$ $\newcommand{\[}[1]{\left[ #1 \right]}$ $\newcommand{\<}[1]{\left< #1 \right>}$ $\newcommand{\|}[1]{\left| #1 \right|}$ $\newcommand{\lbr}[1]{\left\lbrace #1 \right\rbrace}$ $\newcommand{\f}[1]{f \left( #1 \right)}$ $\newcommand{\g}[1]{g \left( #1 \right)}$ $\def\re{\mathcal{Re}}$ $\def\im{\mathcal{Im}}$

In layman's terms, Green's Theorem says that the integral of something over the boundary of a region equals the integral of something else over the region itself. When we talk about the positive orientation on the boundary of $S$, think of taking an arrow tangent to a circle and pointing in the positive direction of the curve, now when we turn it $90^{\circ}$ clockwise it should be pointing out of the circle.



  • $S$: regular region in $\mathbb{R}$

  • $\partial S$: piecewise smooth boundary of $S$

  • $\vec{\mathbf{F}}$: vector field of class $C^1$ on $\bar{S}$



Then: $$\int_{\partial S} \vec{\mathbf{F}} \cdot d\vec{\mathbf{x}} = \iint_S \left( \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x_1} - \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_2} \right) \ dA$$ Now if we let $\vec{\mathbf{F}}= \left( P, Q \right)$ and $\vec{\mathbf{x}} = (x, y)$, then $$\bbox[20px,border:4px groove #1695A3]{\int_{\partial S} P \ dx + Q \ dy = \iint_S \left( \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right) \ dA}$$

Another way to see Green's Theorem is when we take the normal component of $\mathbf{F}$, which is just the tangential component of $\mathbf{F}$ when it's rotated $90^{\circ}$ counterclockwise.
Let $\vec{\mathbf{n(x)}}$ be the unit normal vector to $\partial S$ for all $\vec{\mathbf{x}}$ belonging to $\partial S$, then, $$\int_{\partial S} \vec{\mathbf{F}} \cdot \vec{\mathbf{n}} \ ds = \iint_S \left( \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_1} + \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial x_2} \right)$$ In vector form, Green's theorem looks like $$\int_{\partial S} \vec{\mathbf{F}} \cdot d \vec{\mathbf{x}} = \iint_S \left( \nabla \times \vec{\mathbf{F}} \right) \cdot d \vec{\mathbf{x}}$$
Green's theorem can also be used to find the area of $S$ by way of line integrals. The area of $S$ is given by $$A = \iint_S dA$$ To make it simple, let's conveniently choose
$$ \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 1$$ Now, Green's theorem reduces to
$$\int_{\partial S} P \ dx + Q \ dy = \iint_S 1 \ dA$$ So, $$\int_{\partial S} P \ dx + Q \ dy = A$$ The area of $S$ can then be found by integrating $$A = \frac{1}{2} \int_{\partial S} \left( x \ dy - y \ dx \right)$$ We get this result since $$\frac{d}{dx} \left( \frac{x}{2} \right) = \frac{1}{2} \ \ \mathrm{and} \ \ \frac{d}{dy} \left( -\frac{y}{2} \right) = -\frac{1}{2}$$ So, from above, $$\frac{1}{2} - \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right) = 1$$ Green's theorem has close relationships to Stokes' theorem and the divergence theorem.
