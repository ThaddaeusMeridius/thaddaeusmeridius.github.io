Exponential Form of Complex Numbers

Let $r$ and $\theta$ be polar coordinates of the point $\({x,y}$ that corresponds to a nonzero complex number $z=x+i\,y$. Since $x=r\cos\theta$ and $y=r\sin\theta$, the number $z$ can be written in polar form as $$z=r\({\cos\theta+i\, \sin\theta}$$ $r$ is the length of the radius vector for $z$, in essence, $r=\|{z}$. $\theta$ is the angle $z$ makes with the positive real axis and can be calculated from $$\tan\theta=\frac yx$$ and each value of $\theta$ is called the argument of $z$, where it's set is denoted as $\arg z$. The unique value that lies between $-\pi \leq \Theta \leq \pi$ is called the principle value of $\arg z$ denoted as $\mathrm{Arg}\({z}$. $$\arg z = \mathrm{Arg}\({z} + 2n\pi \qquad \({n=0,\pm1,\pm2,\dots}$$

Example 1

Find the principle argument of the complex number $-1-i$. Solving we find that $$\mathrm{Arg}\({-1-i} = -\frac{3\pi}{4}$$ and $$\arg \({-1-i} = -\frac{3\pi}{4} + 2n\pi \qquad \({n=0,\pm1,\pm2, \dots}$$ Now we can express $z$ in exponential form. The symbol $e^{i\theta}$ is defined by means of Euler's formula as $$e^{i\theta}=\cos\theta + i\, \sin\theta$$ this lets use write $z$ as $$z=re^{i\theta}$$

Roots of Complex Numbers

Two complex numbers $$z_1 = r_1 e^{i\theta_1} \qquad \mathrm{and} \qquad z_2=r_2 e^{i\theta_2}$$ are equal $\iff$ $$r_1=r_2 \qquad \mathrm{and} \qquad \theta_1=\theta_2 + 2k\pi$$ where $k \in \ZZ$ The $n^{\mathrm{th}}$ root of a complex number $z^n$ is a nonzero number $z=re^{i\theta}$ such that $z^n = z_0$ or $$r^n e^{in\theta} = r_0 e^{i\theta_0}$$ as such, from above we see that we must have $$r^n=r_0 \qquad \mathrm{and} \qquad n\theta = \theta_0 + 2k\pi$$ where, as usual, $k \in \ZZ$.
